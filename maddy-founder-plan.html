00

The frame

▸

One unfair advantage drives everything here: the intimacy is not performed. The category is faceless apps doing a warm voice behind a logo. Maddy is a real dad and a real daughter who built this together. Every move below lets that truth do the work a logo cannot.

What winning looks like (and what it is not)

Not view counts. The scoreboard is qualified follows from caregivers and AuDHD adults, practitioner relationships that refer, warm waitlist signups, and Maddy's comfort staying intact. A 1,200-view video that opens one OT conversation beats a 40,000-view video that converts nobody. If you ever feel the pull to optimize for reach, that is the plan failing, not working.

The spine

Jeremy the dad, telling the truth, in short video. Carries the brand with or without Maddy. Start here.

The moat

Practitioners who vouch. No audience or budget needed. The most return per hour. One OT refers across AuDHD, ADHD, and the wider executive-function world.

The multiplier

Maddy's voice at the rung she chooses, and the two of you together, used rarely and powerfully.

The audience, locked

AuDHD is the anchor and the origin: built with an AuDHD co-designer, for AuDHD. The door is wider than the anchor. Maddy is for any brain where starting is the wall: ADHD, autism, TBI and stroke, long COVID, perimenopause, depression, burnout. Canonical line: "AuDHD+ designed to support all executive-functioning needs." The breadth lives in the message, the keywords, and who you talk to. It never lives in the promise. That distinction is the next box.

Three things never move

The honesty boundary. Your story goes anywhere. "Maddy is proven to help X" goes nowhere. Name the mechanism or the research as the subject of any cross-population claim, never Maddy. This is what keeps the wide door safe and the founder credible. It is also what an OT checks before they refer you.

Maddy's consent and comfort. Per piece, revocable, hers. Founder-forward means more of Jeremy's story, not more of her private experience.

The in-product voice. Inside the app it stays built-for-the-user. Founder-forward is the trust wrapper around the product, not a change to the product's voice.

01

The consent operating system

▸

Because Maddy is in this, the system for how she is in it comes before the creative. She is 21, so this is her decision to make and unmake. The child-influencer laws do not apply. What applies is care, by choice. The thing that makes her involvement powerful is that it is real, and real breaks the instant she is pushed. Run her participation the way the app runs its Circle: opt-in, granular, centered on her, revocable.

The choice ladder, with examples

Out
Not this one. No reason needed, costs nothing between you. Example: a problem episode about shutdown days that hits too close this week. She says out, it never comes up again.
Named only
"Co-designed with Maddy." Credit in a caption or lower-third. No likeness, no words. Example: your origin video credits her as co-designer on screen.
Her words
Her perspective, not her face. A line she wrote, a design note in her voice, a voiceover. Example, a card reads "I asked for no red. Red felt like failing."
Her presence
She appears in a piece you lead, approved before it ships. Example: a 20-second clip of the two of you testing a feature, that she watches and okays first.
Her lead
She drives it. Her feature, her story, her voice as the AuDHD adult it was built with. At 21, fully hers to choose. Example: "the three things I asked my dad to change," in her own voice, talking to other AuDHD adults as a peer.

The per-piece flow

  1. Propose. You name the piece, the surface, and the rung you are asking for. One sentence each. "Origin video, TikTok and Reels, asking for named-only."
  2. She reviews the finished asset, not a description. Approving an idea and approving the real cut are different things.
  3. Cooling beat. Anything she is unsure about does not ship that day. She sleeps on it. No same-day pressure, ever.
  4. Two yeses. Hers, and yours as the person protecting her and the brand. Her no always wins. Your safety concern pauses a piece for a conversation; it does not override an adult.
  5. Publish, then log in JER-384 with the rung and her explicit yes.
  6. Revocable. On owned surfaces you can pull it anytime. For a creator reshare, you tell her the honest limit up front so she chooses with real information.

The comment-shield playbook

ND and parenting spaces can be cruel, and Maddy never has to absorb that. Concrete steps:

  • Default: she does not read comments. Agreed up front, not negotiated per video.
  • You or a filter triage. On each piece she is in, you scan first. Hide the cruel ones (hiding is silent; the commenter does not know), block repeat offenders, report harassment.
  • Pre-set the worst-case. Restrict or turn off comments on any piece where she leads, before it posts. You can always open them later.
  • Curated good only. If she wants any feedback, you hand her the handful of warm ones. Never the raw feed.
  • One-tap exit. If a piece turns, you pull it from owned surfaces same day. She knows that is always available.

If she ever wants a creator presence of her own, an AuDHD adult talking to other AuDHD adults as one of them, that is available and it is hers, not a deliverable you assign. As a 21-year-old inside the exact target audience, her peer voice is the most credible content in the entire plan when she wants it. Supported if she wants it, never required.

02

Track: Jeremy the spine

▸

The load-bearing track and the one to start first. You, the dad, telling the truth in short video. It carries the brand whether or not Maddy ever appears, which is exactly why it is the foundation.

Who you are on camera

  • A dad who lived this and built something because nothing else worked.
  • Warm and direct, not warm and soft. Specific, not general.
  • You lead with your life and the problem. The app is the resolution of the story, never the subject of it.
  • No jargon, no lecture. Identity-first language (AuDHDer, autistic, never "person with").
  • The honesty boundary lives here: "the same wall shows up for a lot of brains" is yours to say; "Maddy treats X" is not.

The shooting system (the batch-day runsheet)

Gear, kept deliberately small

  • Your phone, rear camera, 1080p or 4K. A window at 45 degrees for light. A cheap tripod or a stack of books.
  • A clip mic is nice, not required. Quiet room beats fancy mic.
  • That is the whole kit. Production value is not the currency. Honesty and specificity are.

The 90-minute batch (6 to 8 videos)

  • 0-10m Set up: frame chest-up, eyes level with lens, light on your face, check audio with a 5-second test.
  • 10-15m Grab the b-roll bank once: hands, a phone timer, a sink of dishes, a door, morning light, walking, the app screen. Five minutes covers a month of cutaways.
  • 15-80m Film 6 to 8 episodes. One idea each. Say the hook twice (pick the better take later). Do not aim for perfect; aim for true and clear.
  • 80-90m Dump to your editor, label by episode number.

Edit, to a "good enough" bar

  • CapCut or similar. Trim dead air, cut to the hook, add burned-in captions (most people watch muted), one or two b-roll cutaways.
  • Good enough = clear audio, captions, the hook in the first 2 seconds. Ship at good enough. Polish past that is procrastination.

Cadence and the 4-week posting calendar

Two to three posts a week, drawn from one monthly batch. Never daily. A worked example, using the episode numbers from the bank below:

Recognition posts widen the top. Trust posts deepen and convert. Origin and "+" posts carry the story and the breadth. Rotate so no week is all one job.

The call-to-action ladder

  • Most videos: end on "follow for more." Trust before ask.
  • Every third or fourth: "link in bio to join the waitlist." That link is mymaddy.app, which writes to the waitlist, which fires the invite pipeline that is already live. A follow becomes a signup becomes an instant beta invite, no new plumbing.
  • Never: hard-sell a beta to a cold viewer. Cold plus hard ask is the thing that has not worked.
